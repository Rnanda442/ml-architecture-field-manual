Good morning. The central idea of this presentation is simple: a machine-learning architecture should not be chosen because it is fashionable. It should be chosen because its structure matches the information bottleneck in the problem.

By architecture, I mean the organized set of components and information paths inside a model. By information bottleneck, I mean the specific relationship that is difficult to preserve, combine, infer, or move. Sometimes the missing relationship is across time. Sometimes it is across map layers. Sometimes it is between a model’s proposal and an external verification system.

(Point to the title and then the seven case cards.)

We will test that idea in seven very different fields: quantum computing, mineral exploration, finance, materials science, formal mathematics, supply chains, and weather forecasting.

(Ask the audience.)

Before we begin, when you hear the phrase “model weight,” what do you think it means?

(Pause for two or three responses. If no one answers, say: “Most people think it always means a number learned inside the neural network.”)

That is a reasonable answer, but it is incomplete. Today, I will separate three things that are often mixed together. First, the model has parameters learned during training. Second, researchers choose how training errors are weighted. Third, operators choose costs and thresholds after the model predicts. Those three kinds of control do not belong to the same person or the same part of the pipeline.

(Click “New to machine learning? Open the plain-language key.” Point briefly to Input, Model, Architecture, Training, Weight, Prediction, Uncertainty, and Metric. Do not open every question mark.)

This key stays available throughout the presentation. An input is the information given to the model. A prediction is what the model produces before a person or operating system chooses an action. A metric is the measurement used to evaluate performance under a stated test setting. A reported metric is evidence; it is not a guarantee that the system will work everywhere.

3:30–9:15 — CASE 1: QUANTUM ERROR CORRECTION
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

Our first case is quantum error correction from Google Quantum AI. A physical qubit is the real hardware element. It is fragile. A logical qubit is protected information encoded across several physical qubits so that errors can be detected without directly reading and destroying the protected quantum state.

(Point from Input to Architecture to Exact Output to Practical Use.)

The input is a sequence of syndrome events. A syndrome event is warning evidence that a relationship among qubits changed. The architecture is a recurrent transformer. The exact output is a set of logical-error probabilities. Only after those probabilities exist does a controller choose a correction.

Notice the separation: the model estimates the chance of an error. It does not automatically decide the cost of a false correction or a missed error.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck is both spatial and temporal. One warning event does not tell us the full error. The decoder must ask which events across different qubits belong together and whether events from different measurement cycles are part of the same unfolding failure.

(Ask the audience.)

If you heard a smoke alarm once, would that tell you exactly where the fire started and how large it is?

(Pause. Accept one answer.)

No. It is evidence, not a complete diagnosis. Syndrome events work similarly. The architecture must combine scattered evidence before acting.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Syndrome events.”)

We begin with syndrome events across qubits and time. What enters is repeated measurement evidence. What leaves is a structured representation of where and when changes occurred.

(Click “Spatial attention.”)

Spatial attention compares relationships among qubits within the current cycle. Attention is a dynamically calculated influence: for this specific input, one event may strongly affect the interpretation of another. These attention values are not a fixed field policy. They are recalculated from the current syndrome pattern.

(Click “Recurrent memory.”)

Recurrent memory carries unresolved evidence into later measurement cycles. A hidden state is simply the model’s internal summary of useful prior information. If memory fades too quickly, earlier warnings disappear before the complete pattern becomes visible.

(Click “Decoder head,” then “Error probabilities.”)

The decoder head converts the internal representation into logical-error probabilities. Probability here is a calibrated score we hope corresponds to observed frequency over comparable examples. It is not certainty.

(Click “Correction.”)

The correction component belongs to the operational layer. A threshold or cost rule converts probabilities into an action. The model may predict an 85 percent chance of one logical error, while the controller decides whether that probability is high enough to justify correction.

(Point to the related training equation.)

In this equation, theta represents learned model parameters. The lambda values are researcher-selected coefficients controlling how strongly logical-error loss and regularization contribute. Regularization discourages extreme or overly memorized parameter settings. Correction costs are chosen downstream, not learned by this equation.

(Click Lab.)

Try this with me. We will change one control at a time.

(Set Memory retention near 25.)

At low memory retention, older evidence fades before the pattern is complete. The decoder becomes overly dependent on the newest event.

(Move Memory retention near 75. Then move Attention concentration from low to high.)

Now older evidence persists. Raising attention concentration makes a smaller group of qubit relationships dominate. Too diffuse can mix noise; too narrow can miss a distributed pattern.

(Ask the audience.)

Which error would you consider worse: applying an unnecessary correction or missing a real error?

(Take one or two answers. Move the false-correction versus missed-error control while summarizing.)

There is no universal answer. The operational cost setting reflects the consequence structure of the controller.

(Click Result.)

The paper reports lower logical error rate than leading algorithmic decoders on the evaluated real Sycamore surface-code data and generalization beyond the 25-cycle training setting. The boundary is equally important: these are small-code, hardware-specific demonstrations. They do not establish broad deployment readiness for every quantum device.

(Click Next case.)

The quantum case needed memory across space and time. The next case needs representation learning across map layers when labels are scarce.

9:15–15:00 — CASE 2: CRITICAL-MINERAL PROSPECTIVITY
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

Mineral prospectivity means how favorable a location appears for containing a mineral deposit. It is not proof that a deposit exists. The model combines geology, geophysics, and geochemistry, produces a prospectivity ranking, and helps geoscientists prioritize limited field surveys and drilling.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck is weak multimodal evidence under rare labels. Multimodal means that the inputs measure different aspects of the same location. Geology describes rocks and structures. Geophysics measures physical contrasts. Geochemistry measures chemical evidence. Their units, coverage, resolution, and reliability differ.

The positive class—confirmed deposits—is extremely rare. Imagine 20 deposit cells and 9,980 background cells. A useless model that predicts background everywhere would appear 99.8 percent accurate. This is class imbalance: ordinary accuracy can reward a model for ignoring the thing we actually care about.

(Ask the audience.)

If most known deposits occur near roads and older surveys, what accidental pattern might the model learn?

(Pause. If needed, prompt: “Could it learn where people looked rather than where geology is favorable?”)

Exactly. Spatial sampling bias can make exploration history look like geology.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Aligned map layers.”)

The first component aligns geology, magnetics, gravity, and geochemistry into common map patches. A map patch is one local tile used by the model. Alignment matters because layers describing the same location must actually overlap.

(Click “Masked encoder.”)

During self-supervised learning, part of a map patch is hidden, and the encoder tries to reconstruct the missing structure. Self-supervised means the data creates its own learning task; it does not mean humans disappear from the research design. Researchers still choose the mask strategy, data mixture, objective, and validation.

(Click “Rare labels.”)

Fine-tuning introduces the few confirmed deposit labels. Fine-tuning means adapting the broadly pretrained representation to this specific prospectivity task. Rare-positive class weights make missing a known deposit count more strongly during training.

(Click “Prospectivity map,” then “Top targets.”)

The output is a prospectivity and uncertainty map. Uncertainty is not simply one minus prospectivity. A site can have a moderate predicted probability and high uncertainty because evidence is incomplete. The exploration ranking then combines geological prediction with access, survey cost, and field priorities.

(Point to the equation.)

The reconstruction term teaches broad map structure. The prospectivity term uses the scarce labels. The calibration term encourages probabilities that behave honestly across comparable predictions. Their lambda values are researcher-selected. Survey cost belongs after prediction.

(Click Lab. Reduce Geology reliability, raise Magnetic reliability, and leave Geochemistry near the middle.)

The question is which evidence layer the model should trust. These sliders are an illustration, not values reported by the paper. Lowering geology reliability increases uncertainty and changes the priority pattern. Raising magnetic reliability makes magnetic evidence contribute more strongly.

(Raise Rare-deposit class weight. Then raise Survey-cost weight.)

The rare-deposit weight changes the training emphasis. Survey cost changes the operational ranking. Notice that a geologically promising cell can fall in operational priority without its geological prospectivity becoming false.

(Click Result.)

The paper reports that self-supervised pretraining produced more robust features and improved lead-zinc prospectivity predictions across evaluated regions in North America and Australia. The limitation is that known deposits are sparse and spatially biased. Mapping, sampling, and drilling remain the external validation.

(Click Next case.)

Here the architecture learned from abundant unlabeled structure before using scarce labels. Our next architecture learns a sequence of actions whose costs accumulate through time.

15:00–20:30 — CASE 3: DEEP HEDGING
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

Deep hedging is not simply predicting whether a stock price rises tomorrow. A hedge is a position chosen to reduce unwanted exposure. The input is the market and portfolio state. The output is a sequence of hedge actions. The system is evaluated by terminal portfolio risk after transaction costs.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck is sequential action under friction. Every trade changes the next portfolio state and creates cost now. Ten small adjustments might each appear locally useful, but repeated fees and market impact can make the full path worse.

(Ask the audience.)

Would you prefer a model that predicts tomorrow’s direction correctly, or one that leaves you with lower risk after all trading costs?

(Pause.)

For hedging, the second is the actual objective. A locally accurate prediction is not enough.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Market state.”)

The market and portfolio state includes prices, time, holdings, cash, and current exposure.

(Click “Policy.”)

A policy is a state-to-action rule. The policy parameters are learned. Given the current state, it proposes the next hedge position.

(Click “Hedge action,” then “Cost.”)

The action changes the position. Transaction cost represents fees, spread, and market impact associated with changing that position. Cost is part of the environment and decision problem; it is not a price prediction.

(Click “Portfolio,” then “Risk score.”)

The updated portfolio becomes the next input, so the architecture forms a loop. After the final step, a terminal-risk objective evaluates the complete profit-and-loss distribution. Tail risk refers to rare but severe loss.

(Point to the equation.)

Theta represents learned policy parameters. Gamma represents the selected risk preference, and c weights turnover cost. Increasing c discourages frequent trading. Increasing risk aversion can justify paying more transaction cost to reduce severe terminal losses.

(Click Lab. Set Transaction cost low, then high.)

At low transaction cost, the illustrative policy changes positions more often. As cost rises, the model tolerates more exposure rather than paying repeatedly.

(Raise Risk aversion and Stress-scenario weight.)

Risk aversion increases the penalty on damaging uncertainty. Stress-scenario weight makes extreme simulated paths more influential during training.

(Ask the audience.)

What happens if the real market crisis looks nothing like the simulated stress scenarios?

(Pause.)

The learned policy may be confidently wrong. That is simulator mismatch.

(Click Result.)

The paper reports efficient high-dimensional hedging and better terminal convex risk after costs than the standard complete-market solution in its synthetic Heston-market setting. The limitation is that changing regimes, liquidity, and simulator mismatch can make a learned policy brittle.

(Click Next case.)

Deep hedging optimized an action path. The next case generates physical candidates but still needs external physics and laboratory validation.

20:30–26:00 — CASE 4: INVERSE MATERIALS DESIGN
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

Inverse materials design reverses the usual question. Instead of asking what properties a known crystal has, we ask what crystal structure could produce a desired property. MatterGen uses a conditional diffusion model to generate candidate inorganic crystal structures.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck is coupled physical structure. Atom types, atomic coordinates, lattice geometry, and desired properties must describe one consistent object. A candidate can look statistically plausible while being physically unstable.

(Ask the audience.)

If a model draws a realistic-looking bridge, does that prove the bridge can stand?

(Pause.)

No. Generative plausibility is not physical validation. The same distinction matters for crystals.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Noisy crystal.”)

The process begins with noisy atom types, coordinates, and lattice variables. Diffusion generation learns to reverse a gradual noising process.

(Click “Score network.”)

The equivariant score network predicts how to remove noise while respecting geometric transformations. Equivariant means that rotating or translating the crystal changes the internal representation in a physically consistent way rather than changing the underlying identity arbitrarily.

(Click “Channel balance.”)

Atom, coordinate, and lattice losses must be balanced. If coordinate error dominates the objective, the model may improve positions while neglecting incorrect atom identities or lattice geometry. The lambda values determine how these training errors compete.

(Click “Guidance.”)

Property conditioning tells the model what type of candidate is requested. Guidance strength changes how aggressively generation follows that target. Stronger guidance can improve target alignment but may reduce diversity or produce unrealistic structures.

(Click “Candidate,” then “Triage.”)

The output is an ordered candidate crystal, not a confirmed material. Simulation and synthesis triage decide which candidates deserve density-functional-theory calculations and laboratory work.

(Click Lab. Raise Guidance strength, then separately move Atom-channel weight, Coordinate-channel weight, and Lattice-channel weight.)

Move one channel at a time. Each setting changes what kind of reconstruction error receives emphasis. This illustrates why one aggregate loss number can hide a failure in one part of a structured object.

(Ask the audience.)

Would you spend an expensive synthesis budget on the candidate with the highest model score alone?

(Pause.)

The safer answer is no. Use model score for triage, then require physics-based screening.

(Click Result.)

The paper reports candidates more than twice as likely to be stable, unique, and new than prior models, more than ten times closer to a local energy minimum, and a synthesized material within 20 percent of its target property. The limitation is that DFT and synthesis remain outside the generator and can reject plausible samples.

(Click Next case.)

The materials case separated generation from physical validation. The theorem case makes the same separation even sharper: plausible language is not formal correctness.

26:00–31:30 — CASE 5: FORMAL THEOREM PROVING
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

This system takes a formal theorem, the current proof state, and available lemmas. A language model proposes Lean proof steps, a search procedure explores branches, and the Lean verifier accepts only formally valid steps.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck is proposal versus correctness. A language model can produce a highly plausible continuation that is formally invalid. Probability ranks what to try; verification decides what is correct.

(Ask the audience.)

If a mathematical explanation sounds convincing, is that the same as every step being formally valid?

(Pause.)

No. That difference is the reason this architecture includes both a generator and a verifier.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Proof state.”)

The proof state describes what has already been established and what goals remain.

(Click “Model proposes.”)

The language model proposes tactics, lemmas, or subgoals. Its learned parameters rank likely continuations. Likelihood is useful for prioritization but is not a proof certificate.

(Click “Search tree.”)

Branching proof search keeps several possibilities alive. The branch budget is the finite number of attempts assigned across those paths. Spending the entire budget on one plausible but wrong branch can hide a lower-probability valid proof.

(Click “Verifier.”)

Lean checks each formal step exactly under the encoded rules and definitions. The verifier is external to the language model’s confidence.

(Click “Accepted proof,” then “Retry/prune.”)

An accepted branch becomes a formal proof artifact. Rejected branches are pruned or retried, and verifier feedback changes where search effort goes next.

(Point to the equation.)

The supervised term teaches from existing formal proofs. The reinforcement term uses advantage, meaning whether an outcome was better or worse than expected. KL control discourages the updated policy from moving too far from a reference model. Beta and kappa are researcher-selected; theta is learned.

(Click Lab. Select a promising branch and set a small Proof-attempt budget. Then raise the budget.)

With a small budget, a wrong high-probability branch can consume the search. A larger budget explores more alternatives, but computation increases.

(Ask the audience.)

Should the model’s confidence ever override a verifier rejection?

(Pause.)

In formal proof, no. Confidence ranks attempts; the verifier determines acceptance.

(Click Result.)

DeepSeek-Prover-V2 reports an 88.9 percent pass ratio on MiniF2F-test and 49 of 658 PutnamBench problems solved. Those are benchmark results under finite search budgets. Success still depends on formalization quality, available libraries, search budget, and benchmark scope.

(Click Next case.)

Formal search outputs a verified artifact. The next system outputs a probability distribution, and a business cost rule chooses an action from that distribution.

31:30–37:00 — CASE 6: PROBABILISTIC DEMAND FORECASTING
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

DeepAR predicts future demand across many products. Instead of producing only one best guess, it produces a probability distribution. A distribution describes multiple possible demand levels and their relative likelihoods.

(Click Bottleneck.)

The bottleneck combines shared sequence memory with uncertainty. One model per product wastes common patterns, especially when individual series are sparse. A point forecast also hides the difference between likely demand and extreme but costly outcomes.

(Ask the audience.)

If the best guess is 100 units, should the business always order exactly 100?

(Pause.)

Not necessarily. The answer depends on forecast uncertainty and whether shortage or overstock is more costly.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Histories.”)

The inputs are product histories and covariates. A covariate is additional information related to demand, such as price, promotion, season, or holiday timing.

(Click “Shared RNN.”)

The shared recurrent core learns patterns across many products. Autoregressive means the model uses previous observed or predicted values to help forecast the next value.

(Click “Distribution head.”)

The likelihood head converts the hidden state into parameters of a probability distribution. Likelihood measures how well the chosen distribution explains observed demand during training.

(Click “Forecast fan.”)

The forecast fan shows quantiles such as P10, P50, and P90. A quantile is a position within the predicted distribution. P50 is the median: half the predicted probability lies below it and half above it. P90 is a higher-demand planning point, not a 90 percent accurate forecast.

(Click “Cost quantile,” then “Inventory.”)

The business-cost quantile is chosen after prediction. If shortage is much more expensive than overstock, the decision moves toward a higher quantile. The final inventory quantity is an action, not a hidden neural-network output.

(Point to the equation.)

Theta represents learned recurrent and distribution-head parameters. Lambda weights decide how forecast horizons contribute to training. The ratio q uses shortage cost over total shortage plus overstock cost. Increasing shortage cost raises q and usually raises the selected inventory quantity.

(Click Lab. Set Shortage cost to 7 and Overstock cost to 3.)

The selected quantile rises above the median because stocking out is more expensive.

(Reverse the values: Shortage 3, Overstock 7.)

Now the decision moves lower because leftover inventory is more expensive.

(Ask the audience.)

Did the model’s forecast distribution change when I changed the costs?

(Pause.)

No. The operational decision changed. This is the cleanest example of why prediction and action must be kept separate.

(Click Result.)

The paper reports forecasting-accuracy improvements of around 15 percent over then-state-of-the-art methods across several real-world datasets. Tail calibration can still fail when covariates are poor, distribution assumptions are mismatched, or demand enters a new regime.

(Click Next case.)

Demand forecasting shares patterns across products and time. Our final case moves information across a spherical, multiscale representation of the entire atmosphere.

37:00–43:00 — CASE 7: GRAPHCAST WEATHER FORECASTING
============================================================

(Click Objective.)

GraphCast reads two recent global weather states, predicts the next atmospheric state six hours ahead, and repeatedly rolls that forecast forward. The architecture is a message-passing graph neural network built on a spherical multimesh.

(Click Bottleneck.)

Weather is local and global at the same time. A front requires fine regional detail, while planetary waves connect distant locations. A latitude-longitude grid also represents physical area unevenly near the poles and equator.

(Ask the audience.)

If every location could communicate only with its immediate neighbor, what would happen when information needed to travel across the globe?

(Pause.)

It would require many processing steps. Too many steps can also over-smooth the representation, meaning distinct locations become too similar.

(Click Component walkthrough. Click “Weather grid.”)

The input is two recent global weather grids plus graph geometry. A weather state includes variables such as wind, pressure, temperature, and humidity across locations.

(Click “Encode graph.”)

The grid-to-mesh encoder converts grid values into features on graph nodes and edges. A node is one computational location. An edge is an allowed information path.

(Click “Message passing.”)

Message passing lets each node receive, transform, and combine information from connected nodes. Fine edges preserve local detail. Coarse edges allow information to travel farther in fewer steps. Multimesh means multiple connection scales coexist.

(Click “Decode grid.”)

The mesh-to-grid decoder turns processed graph features back into the next regular weather grid.

(Click “Next 6h state,” then “Rollout/use.”)

The next six-hour state becomes input to the next step. That repeated process is an autoregressive rollout. Four steps advance one day; repeated steps build the medium-range forecast. Operational warning thresholds and false-alarm costs remain downstream.

(Point to the equation.)

Theta represents learned network parameters. Lambda balances variables so one quantity or unit scale does not dominate training. Latitude weight omega corrects geographic area imbalance. Gradients update theta; researchers select the objective weights.

(Click Lab. Compare local-only connections with multiscale connections. Raise Message-passing depth.)

Coarse connections move information farther at the same depth. Increasing depth too far raises the risk of over-smoothing.

(Increase Rollout step.)

Longer rollout means one-step errors can accumulate because each forecast becomes a later input.

(Ask the audience.)

If GraphCast produces one very accurate trajectory, does that automatically tell us the probability of an extreme event?

(Pause.)

No. GraphCast is deterministic in this formulation. A fast, accurate trajectory is not a complete uncertainty distribution.

(Click Result.)

The Science paper reports lower error than HRES on more than 90 percent of 1,380 evaluated targets and a ten-day global forecast at quarter-degree resolution in under one minute. The limitation is that operational uncertainty still requires ensembles, calibration, or other downstream systems, especially for rare extremes and warnings.

43:00–48:00 — SUPPLEMENTS: REUSABLE MECHANISMS
============================================================

(Click Supplements in the top navigation.)

The seven flagship cases use recurring architectural ideas. These supplements separate those reusable mechanisms from the paper-specific stories.

(Click RNN.)

A vanilla recurrent neural network carries one hidden state through time. The same transition weights are reused at every step. It is simple, but long-term information can fade or unstable gradients can grow.

(Move the Memory strength slider once.)

This is the basic recurrence idea used in sequence problems: current input plus retained state produces the next state.

(Click LSTM.)

An LSTM adds a separate cell state and three gates. The forget gate decides what prior memory remains. The input gate decides what new information is written. The output gate decides what memory is exposed.

(Click GRU, then click “RNN/LSTM/GRU compare.”)

A GRU uses reset and update gates in a more compact state. The comparison is not that one architecture is universally best. The design question is how much explicit memory control the problem requires and what complexity the data can support.

(Ask the audience.)

Which flagship cases depended most directly on sequence memory?

(Pause. Expected answers include quantum error correction and demand forecasting.)

(Click Actor-Critic.)

Actor-critic separates action selection from value estimation. The actor chooses an action. The critic estimates expected future value. An advantage signal asks whether the outcome was better or worse than the critic expected. A biased critic can push the actor in the wrong direction.

(Click Formal Search.)

Formal search separates probability, budget, and verification. Model probability ranks proof moves. Search budget allocates attempts. The verifier alone determines formal acceptance.

(Click Self-Supervised.)

Self-supervised pretraining hides part of an unlabeled input, learns to reconstruct it, and then fine-tunes the encoder with scarce labels. The important limitation is task alignment: becoming good at reconstruction does not guarantee the representation is useful for the downstream scientific question.

48:00–51:00 — CONCLUSION AND FINAL AUDIENCE QUESTION
============================================================

Across all seven cases, the architecture followed the bottleneck.

Quantum decoding needed relationships across qubits and time, so it combined attention with recurrence. Mineral prospectivity had abundant unlabeled maps and scarce deposits, so it used masked pretraining and fine-tuning. Deep hedging needed a sequence of actions evaluated after costs, so it learned a policy over the full path. MatterGen needed coupled geometric generation, so it used equivariant diffusion and separate loss channels. Formal theorem proving needed plausible proposals separated from exact correctness, so it combined a language model, search, and a verifier. DeepAR needed shared sequence patterns and uncertainty, so it produced a forecast distribution before the business chose a quantity. GraphCast needed local and global communication on a curved Earth, so it used multiscale graph message passing.

(Pause, then ask the final question.)

If you were evaluating a new machine-learning system tomorrow, what would you ask first: “Which model is most advanced?” or “What information relationship is the current approach failing to capture?”

(Invite two responses.)

The second question is usually more useful. It directs attention to the scientific or operational bottleneck before architecture selection.

There is one final distinction I want you to retain. The model learns parameters. Researchers choose data handling and training priorities. Operators choose costs, thresholds, and actions. A clear technical explanation shows who controls each value, what changes when it moves, and what external validation still remains.

(Point to the current limitation or source link.)

Every reported result today belongs to a specific paper, dataset, metric, and test setting. None of the classroom sliders reproduces a paper experiment. The labs expose relationships so we can reason about them. The paper sources and limitations define what the evidence actually supports.

Thank you. I am happy to go back to any case, equation, or architecture component for questions.

OPTIONAL 3–5 MINUTE QUESTION BUFFER
============================================================

If the audience asks what a weight means:

(Open the Weight key.)

A learned parameter is stored inside the model and updated by gradients. A dynamic weight, such as attention, is calculated for the current input. A data or loss weight is chosen by researchers to control training emphasis. A decision cost or threshold is chosen after prediction to convert information into action.

If the audience asks what a gradient means:

A gradient indicates how a small change in a learned parameter would change the training loss. The optimizer uses that information to update parameters. The gradient does not choose the scientific goal, the field budget, the inventory cost, or the warning policy.

If the audience asks whether the equations are complete reproductions:

The equations on this site are compact teaching representations. They identify the important ownership and tradeoffs, but they do not reproduce every implementation detail in the original code or paper. The primary paper links define the complete research method.

If the audience asks which architecture is best:

There is no universal winner. An architecture is appropriate when its inductive structure matches the bottleneck, its output supports the real decision, and its performance survives external validation under the intended setting.
